I told them the seals were failing. I told them in writing, three times, over four months. They told me I was "not a team player." Then they fired me and put in the paperwork that I quit.

My name is Gloria Banks. I'm fifty-two, and for eleven years I was the safety and compliance manager at a parts manufacturer outside Chicago. My job — my entire job — was to notice problems before they became disasters. I was good at it. I was good at it right up until being good at it made me inconvenient.

The problem was a seal. A component in a product line that thousands of units depended on. I ran the numbers, I saw the failure rate creeping, and I did exactly what I was hired to do. I documented it. I emailed my boss, Dale Foster. I flagged it to the line. I put it in the quarterly report in language no one could misread.

Dale's response, more or less, was that I worried too much and that the numbers I was "obsessing over" were going to scare the client.

I kept flagging it. Each time a little more formally. Each time he got a little colder.

And then one Friday, HR called me in and told me my position was being eliminated. Restructuring, they said. They slid a folder across the desk. And in that folder, the separation paperwork described my departure as a voluntary resignation.

I had not resigned. I would never have resigned. I have a mortgage and a kid in college and a job I was good at.

But "voluntary resignation" meant no unemployment fight, no wrongful-termination claim, no severance dispute. It meant I disappeared quietly and the seal problem disappeared with me.

What Dale Foster forgot is that a compliance manager keeps records the way other people breathe.

So I sued. Wrongful termination, retaliation for protected safety reporting. And last week I sat down across a long polished table in a downtown law office for a deposition, with a court reporter at the end and my attorney, Joel Stern, beside me.

Across from me sat Curtis Hale, the company's lawyer. Pinstripe suit. The kind of smirk that's been practiced in a mirror. He leaned back in his chair like the outcome was already decided, and he walked me through their version: that I was difficult, that I underperformed, that I chose to leave.

"Ms. Banks," he said, almost bored, "isn't it true that no one ever told you your reports were unwelcome? That this is, frankly, a story you've constructed after the fact?"

I folded my hands on the table. I stayed calm. I'd waited a long time to be this calm.

I stopped sleeping. I'd lie awake running failure rates in my head, doing the math on how many units were in the field, picturing the one that fails at the wrong moment. I wasn't difficult. I was right. There's a difference, and Dale Foster spent four months pretending he couldn't tell them apart.

When they walked me out with a box and a "voluntary resignation" form, the worst part wasn't losing the job. It was the quiet little voice that said maybe you imagined it. Maybe you really were the problem. Maybe you made too much of a seal.
