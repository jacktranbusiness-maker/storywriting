She told me once about the night that made her swear the oath she finally kept: a partner at her old firm, decades ago, who told her to "find a real solution for the baby situation" the week her son was hospitalized with a fever that wouldn't break. "It took me thirty years to build a firm where I could keep that promise," she told me. "And then I almost let a man like Grant make me forget I'd made it. You reminded me. So thank you."

I hadn't done anything but refuse to apologize for my daughter. But maybe that was the whole thing. Maybe the only way these chains ever break is when one tired person finally says, out loud, in a marble lobby, that they will not be ashamed of loving their child.

Grant was right about one thing, in a way he never meant. My daughter's heart was, in fact, the firm's problem.

It turned out to be the problem that finally made the firm better.

I thought Ethan had chosen me.

He'd chosen the access he assumed came with marrying a girl who turned out to be richer than he could imagine — and then, having never checked, decided to rob a fortune he didn't even know existed by stealing a single apartment.

When people are certain you're powerless, let them say everything. They always tell you exactly how they planned to win.

And then you simply make sure they were wrong.
