I polished that credenza every Saturday for forty-three years.

I never told anyone what was inside it. Thomas asked me not to, and I honored that, because when you love someone for that long you learn which promises are the ones that hold the shape of a life together. This was one of those promises.

My name is Margaret Ellison. I am sixty-eight years old. I have lived in this house in Charleston, South Carolina since 1983. I raised two sons in this house. I buried my husband from this house six weeks ago. And for the past three Saturdays, I have sat in this living room while my sons argued about what to sell.

I want to tell you about the credenza first.

It is a 1920s American walnut piece — four drawers, brass hardware, a beveled mirror panel on the back that Thomas's mother used to check her lipstick before answering the front door. It weighs more than it looks like it should. The wood has a warmth that only comes from decades of oil and cloth and the kind of care that doesn't announce itself.

There is a hidden drawer. It sits behind the lowest panel, accessible only if you pull the bottom drawer fully out and reach into the gap behind it. Thomas showed me the drawer on our second anniversary. He said his mother had kept love letters there during the war. He said: "One day I'll put something in there for you."

I never opened it without him asking me to. I didn't need to. I trusted the promise.
