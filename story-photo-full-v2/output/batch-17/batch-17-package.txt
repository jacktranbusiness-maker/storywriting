I chose to keep my promise to a dying woman, which meant staying in their lives exactly as much as that promise required and not one inch in a way that confused anyone about who was who.

I helped Marcus set up the recordings the way Elena wanted. We didn't make a shrine. We didn't save them for some terrible future "when she's old enough to understand." We did what Elena asked: we made her ordinary. From the very first night, Elena's voice was just Sol's bedtime. One of the forty-seven lullabies, every night, the same as any mother singing her baby down. Sol doesn't remember a time before her mother's voice was the last thing she heard before sleep, because there wasn't one.

That was four years ago.

Sol is four now. She is sunlight, exactly as named. And here is the thing that makes me believe Elena was the wisest person I ever met: Sol is not sad about her mother. How could she be? Her mother sings to her every night. Her mother says "good morning, my love" from a little speaker some mornings when she's having a hard day. To Sol, her mom isn't a tragedy or a photograph or a grave. Her mom is a voice that loves her, that has always loved her, that was loving her before she was even born. Elena made herself ordinary, and in doing that, she made herself permanent.

I asked Marcus once if he worried it would confuse her someday — a mother she only knows as a voice. He shook his head. He said the confusing thing would have been the other version: a mother who was only ever a silence, a sad story whispered over her head, a name people went quiet around. "Elena didn't leave Sol a ghost," he said. "She left her a lullaby. There's a difference, and Sol gets to grow up on the right side of it."

Marcus, when Sol asks where Mommy is, tells her the true thing they decided together: "Mommy got very sick and her body had to stop, but she loved you so much that she made sure you'd always have her voice. That's her, singing. She recorded it just for you, before you were even born, because she couldn't wait to love you."

I'm still in their lives. Marcus and I are family now, in the strange beautiful way grief stitches people together. I'm "Tía Bianca." I'm the lady who carried Sol and who keeps the stories about her mother. On Sol's birthday every year, the three of us — Marcus, Sol, and me — listen to the recording Elena made for "your first birthday, my love," and Marcus and I cry and Sol thinks it's a normal thing that all kids do, and in our family it is.

The bittersweet never fully leaves. It's not supposed to. There's a grief in it I'll carry forever — for the hour that should have been a lifetime, for a woman who wanted a baby for nine years and got nine days with her. Some nights it sits on my chest. I let it. Elena earned my grief; I won't pretend she didn't.

But she also taught me what the agency rep, with all her professional guidance about managing my attachment, never understood:

Love is not outside the scope. Love is the whole scope. The contract is just the paperwork that lets the love happen.

The agency uses Elena and Sol as a "case study" now — anonymized, of course — in their training, as an example of "navigating complex emotional outcomes." Marcus laughed when he heard that, the sad kind of laugh. The same agency that sat me down and told me to keep my professional distance now teaches my refusal to do it as best practice. They got there eventually. People usually do — once somebody goes first and shows them the cost of the careful version.

I was hired to carry a baby and hand her over and step back cleanly.

I chose, instead, to carry a dying woman's last wish, and to keep it, and to press play every night for a little girl who falls asleep, ordinary and safe, to the sound of a mother who loved her into the world and made sure she'd never have to lose the voice.

Forty-seven lullabies.

One for nearly every night of the week and then some, so they could rotate and never wear thin — because Elena thought of everything, even the quiet math of a child's bedtime stretching across years she knew she wouldn't get to see.

We've never once run out.
