I have to confess something I've never told anyone: I've lived in that "abandoned" house for fifty-one years. So when a young man in a work vest unlocked the front door, called it his, and started measuring my home for demolition, I came down the attic stairs and asked him to sit down. There was something about my late husband he needed to hear.

My name is Eleanor Voss.

I'm eighty-one. The house is a grand old Victorian in Providence, the kind people call a "fixer-upper" now, with boarded windows and peeling paint and a wraparound porch that sags on one side. To the city, to the bank, to the young man with the clipboard, it looked abandoned.

It was never abandoned. I was always inside it.

My husband Walter and I bought that house in 1962, when we were young and the neighborhood was different and a steelworker and a seamstress could still buy a beautiful old home if they were willing to fix it forever. We never had children. We had each other, and we had that house, and Walter spent forty years repairing it board by board with his own hands.

Walter died eleven years ago. The house went quiet in a way I can't describe. But I stayed, because every room held him — the banister he sanded, the window seat he built me, the kitchen where we danced badly to the radio. Leaving would have been leaving him.

Walter was a steelworker with careful hands and a worried heart. He grew up watching his own mother get put out of her home after his father died — no say, no warning, just a knock at the door and nowhere to go. It marked him in a way he carried his whole life. He used to say a house isn't walls, it's the promise that you'll always have somewhere to stand.

We poured ourselves into that Victorian for forty years. He sanded the banister by hand until it shone. He built me a window seat so I could read in the afternoon light. We danced badly in the kitchen to a radio that only got two stations. We never had children — it wasn't in the cards for us — so the house became the thing we raised together, room by room, year by year.

When Walter died eleven years ago, I couldn't leave. Every board held him.

Over the years, things got hard. The money Walter left thinned out. I let the outside go because I couldn't climb a ladder anymore. I closed off the rooms I couldn't heat and lived in a few warm ones and the attic, where the light is best. From the street, with the boards and the peeling paint, the house looked dead.

I'm not too proud to admit I let myself disappear right along with the house. When the person you fixed the whole world with is gone, the world stops seeming worth fixing.

There were tax troubles I didn't fully understand — letters I should have answered and didn't, because answering mail is hard when you're grieving and old and the letters are frightening. Somewhere in all that, the city moved to auction the house for back taxes. I didn't know what an auction even meant until it was over.

One afternoon I heard a key in the front door.

A young man came in. Work vest, tape measure on his belt, a clipboard in his hand. He walked into my foyer like he owned it — because, he believed, he did. He'd won the house at a courthouse auction. He started measuring the walls, talking out loud about "gutting this whole floor" and "opening up the kitchen."

The kitchen where Walter and I danced.

I came to the top of the attic stairs, and the young man nearly jumped out of his skin. I imagine I was quite a sight — a small old woman in a hand-knit cardigan, appearing out of a house he'd been told was empty.
