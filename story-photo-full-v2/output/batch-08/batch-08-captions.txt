I've carried the same tiny baby photo my whole life — the only thing that came with me when I was adopted at birth. Last month I walked into an open casting call on a total whim, and a stranger in the waiting room looked up, and I was staring at my own face.

My name is Jess Lindqvist.

I'm twenty-nine. I was adopted as a newborn by a wonderful couple in Wisconsin who gave me everything but the one thing they couldn't: an answer to where I came from. The adoption was closed. The records were sealed. All I had — all that came with me — was a single small photograph of an infant, me, taken the day I was born, and a tiny locket, empty, that the agency said had been left with me.

I'm not going to pretend I spent my life in agony about it. My parents loved me, my childhood was happy. But there's a particular loneliness to not having a face that looks like yours anywhere in the world. Every family photo, I was the one who matched no one. I used to look at strangers and wonder, idly, painfully, are you mine?

That photo and that little locket were the entire museum of where I came from. Two objects. I'd studied them my whole life like they might one day confess something — a photo of a baby who could have been anyone, and an empty locket with a small dent on the back, as if it had once been part of something larger that broke in two.

I keep the baby photo in my wallet. I touch the locket when I'm nervous — turn it on its chain, a habit since childhood I've never been able to break.

I should explain what that particular loneliness is like, because people who grew up surrounded by their own blood don't always get it. It's not sadness, exactly. It's a low, constant hum of unbelonging. You blow out birthday candles and wonder whose nose you have. You fill out the medical forms that ask for family history and write "unknown, adopted" in every box, and the word "unknown" starts to feel like it's describing you, not just your records. You love your parents completely and still feel, somewhere underneath, like a single puzzle piece that wandered into the wrong box.

I'd looked, a little, over the years. The records were sealed tight. I'd sent a cheek swab to one of those ancestry sites and gotten a list of fourth cousins who never wrote back. Eventually I made a kind of peace with not knowing. Or I told myself I had.

Last month, on a complete whim, I went to an open casting call in Madison. They wanted "real people," background types, for some local production, and a friend dared me, and I had a free afternoon, and honestly I think part of me just wanted to do something spontaneous.

I almost left before I even signed in. The hallway outside the casting room was packed — a long row of folding chairs full of hopefuls, that fluorescent waiting-room buzz. I stood there with my baby photo still in my hand from showing my friend, feeling silly.

And then a woman a few chairs down looked up.

The hallway went away.

She had my face.

Not similar. Not "you two could be related." My face. My eyes. The exact set of my mouth. The little crease I get between my eyebrows. I felt the floor tilt. I've spent twenty-nine years scanning every crowd for a face like mine and never finding one, and here, under ugly fluorescent lights, at a casting call I almost skipped, was a stranger wearing it.

She stood up slowly, like she was as dizzy as I was.

And then I watched her hand drift up to her throat — to a small locket on a chain. The same nervous habit I've had my whole life. She turned it between her fingers exactly the way I turn mine.

I looked down at the locket in my own hand.

They were the same. Two halves of one thing.

Neither of us said a word for what felt like an hour. The whole hallway had gone quiet, everyone's heads turning back and forth between two women with one face, both of us clutching identical lockets, both of us starting to cry without knowing yet exactly why.

Then she whispered the question that has no business being asked in a casting-call hallway, the question I'd wondered my whole life.

"Were you," she said, "...were you adopted?"

When my little sister found out her cancer was terminal, she didn't cry for herself. She held my hand and asked if I would carry her baby — so that a piece of her could stay in the world after she was gone. I said yes. I had no idea she was asking me for something even bigger.

My name is Cora Banks.

I'm thirty-eight. My sister Lena was thirty-three, and she was the bright one, the warm one, the one who lit up rooms while I held them up from the corner. She and her husband Tom had been trying for a baby for years. They'd just started fertility treatment — they had embryos, frozen, full of hope — when the headaches started.

Lena and I were five years apart and opposite in every way. She was sunshine — loud, warm, the kind of person strangers told their life stories to in grocery lines. I was the quiet one, the careful one, the one who remembered everyone's allergies and drove everyone to the airport. Our whole lives, she shone and I steadied. I never resented it. Holding up the room so my little sister could light it was the most natural thing in the world to me. It was just who we were.

She married Tom, who adored her the way she deserved, and they wanted a baby so badly it ached to watch them try, month after month, year after year. They'd finally turned to fertility treatment. They had embryos frozen, a whole future on ice, the day the headaches started and didn't stop.

The diagnosis was the kind that doesn't come with a long road. Terminal. Months, not years. Lena was thirty-three.

I remember the day she told me the cancer meant she'd never carry her own child. That was the thing that finally broke her — not her own death, but that the baby she and Tom dreamed of would die frozen with her. She sat in my kitchen and wept like I'd never seen her weep.

I sat with her through the appointments where they explained what was coming. She took the news about her own life with a steadiness that broke me in half. The only time she truly fell apart was over the baby she'd never get to raise — the child she and Tom had wanted for so many years, now frozen, waiting, with no one to carry it into the world.

And then, a few weeks later, calm in a way that scared me, she took my hand and asked.

She asked if I would carry the baby. Their embryo. So that a piece of her — her and Tom's child, their hope — could be born into the world even after she'd left it.

I said yes before she finished the sentence. Of course I said yes. She was my little sister. I'd spent my whole life holding up the rooms she lit. If my body could give the world one more thing that was hers, then my body was hers.

The doctors said it was possible. We moved fast, because we had to, because Lena was on a clock none of us could stop. The transfer worked. I got pregnant with my sister's baby, and for a few months we had something almost like joy in the middle of the grief — Lena, thin and luminous in her headscarf, pressing her hand to my growing belly, feeling for kicks, naming the baby, planning a life she knew, and I knew, she would probably never see.

We didn't say that last part out loud. We just lived in the small bright window we had.

I thought I understood what I'd agreed to. I'd carry the baby, give birth, and hand my niece or nephew to Tom and to whatever time Lena had left. I thought that was the whole of it.

But near the end, in a quiet hospital room with the soft light coming through the window, Lena took my hand and rested it with hers on my belly, and she asked me to promise her something. Something I didn't fully understand in the moment. There was a folded letter on the nightstand, in her handwriting, that she told me not to read until "after."

"Promise me, Cora," she whispered. "Whatever happens. Promise me."

I promised. I'd have promised her anything.

I didn't understand what I'd promised until the letter — and until Lena was gone, and the baby came, and her last wish turned out to be so much bigger than carrying a child.
