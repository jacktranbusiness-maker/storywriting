But it is full — full of a four-year-old's laugh that sounds exactly like her mother's, full of a house where Lena is spoken of every single day, full of more love than I ever once let myself hope for from my corner.

Here's the thing about a last wish, the thing I've learned holding my sister's daughter through four years of bedtimes.

The biggest ones don't ask you to do something. They ask you to become someone. My sister didn't spend her last strength making me a courier for her child. She spent it cracking open the corner I'd hidden in my whole life and pushing me, gently, fiercely, out into the middle of the room.

I am a mother now. I am loved out loud, every single day, by a four-year-old with her mother's eyebrows who has no memory of me ever being anything else. The woman who held up every room from the shadows lives in the brightest one she's ever known.

Lena gave me that. From a hospital bed, thin and luminous, with her hand on my belly and a folded letter on the nightstand, my little sister's last wish was to finally take care of the sister who'd spent her whole life taking care of everyone else.

I promised her I'd be Lena Rose's mother. I promised her I'd let myself be loved.

I keep both promises every morning, when a small voice that sounds exactly like my sister's calls down the hall for Mama.

I answer to it now.

That was the wish. And it came true.
