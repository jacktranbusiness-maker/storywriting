I spent fourteen months as a captive. I won't go into all of it. There are parts I'm still not ready to say out loud. But I survived. And eventually, through a series of events I'm still grateful for every single day, I was recovered and brought home.

The Army was still untangling the records when I landed back in Georgia. The paperwork said I was dead. There was a death certificate with my name on it. And there was, I learned, a memorial service already scheduled — because my family had spent fourteen months believing they'd buried me, and they were finally holding the service my mother hadn't been strong enough to hold until now.

I asked them not to call ahead.

Looking back, maybe that was selfish. Maybe I should have let someone prepare her. But I'd spent fourteen months dreaming of my mother's face, and I could not bear the idea of telling her over a phone, through an official, secondhand. I needed to see her. I needed her to see me.

So I put on my dress uniform — it hung loose on me now; I'd lost so much weight that the man in the mirror looked like a stranger — and I drove to the post chapel near Fort Benning.

I slipped in the back as the service was already underway.

The chapel was full. Folding chairs. Mourners in black. An easel at the front with a framed photo of me — a photo from before, when my face was fuller and my eyes hadn't seen what they'd seen.

And at the front, an honor guard soldier in full dress was presenting a tightly folded flag to my mother.

Margaret Carter. Fifty-eight. In a black dress. Her hands trembling as she reached out to take the flag that's given to the families of the fallen.

The flag for me.

I stood at the back, cap in my hands, and I could not move. I could not speak. I watched my mother receive a folded flag for a son who was standing forty feet behind her, alive.

The grief in that room was a physical thing. I could feel it. Fourteen months of it, gathered into one chapel on one afternoon.

And somewhere in my chest, joy and horror were fighting each other so hard I thought I'd come apart.

Because I was alive. I was home. I was about to give my mother the greatest gift a grieving parent could ever receive.

But I was also about to walk into the middle of a funeral and undo fourteen months of mourning in a single breath — and I had no idea what that would do to the woman holding my flag.

I should tell you about the flag, because it's the part I can't stop thinking about.

The flag they were folding for me was real. The grief in that room was real. My mother's trembling hands were real. There's a joy in coming back from the dead. But there's a grief in it too, a strange one nobody warns you about: grief for the months you were gone, for the version of your family that broke while you were away, for the simple ordinary life that the war and the captivity stole from all of you.

I stood at the back of that chapel, gaunt in a uniform that no longer fit, and I felt both at once, so hard I could barely stand.

A little girl in a pew near the back turned around first. She saw me. Her eyes went wide.

They called me the broke regular. Every night for three years. They had no idea who actually owned the room.

My name is Sal Moretti. I'm sixty-three years old. Rumpled cardigan. Reading glasses on a cord. I look like somebody's tired uncle, and that's exactly the point.

For three years, I sat at the same back-room poker game on the outskirts of Atlantic City. A converted warehouse lounge. Green felt under a low pendant light. The kind of private game that runs late and plays for real money.

Dex is forty-five. Gold chains. Silk shirts. The kind of man who confuses cruelty with charisma. He ran the floor, set the stakes, took the rake. And he decided early on that I was an easy target.

"Sal's here," he'd announce when I walked in. "Everybody hide your wallets, the broke regular needs a loan." He'd laugh. The table would laugh, because Dex controlled the table.

That I own.

I don't have much. I clean offices at night. I raise my little girl alone — she's two, and she goes everywhere with me because childcare costs more than I make. I sing to her every night. She falls asleep to my voice. People have told me my whole life that I have a gift.
