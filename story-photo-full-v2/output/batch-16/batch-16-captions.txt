Thirties. A buttoned coat against the cold. A document folder held tight against her chest. She was watching us climb — watching Andrei specifically — with an expression I couldn't read at first. It wasn't anger, exactly. It was something heavier. Resolve, maybe. Grief.

I felt Andrei's hand go tense in mine.

I felt it before I understood it.

His steps slowed. His tight smile got tighter. And his hand — the hand I was holding, the hand I was about to put a ring on — started, very slightly, to slip out of mine.

"Andrei?" the woman said. Quiet. Steady.

"Andrei," she said again. "You're getting married."

It wasn't a question.

I looked between them. "Andrei, who is this?"

He opened his mouth. Nothing came out.

So the woman answered for him.

"My name is Oksana," she said, looking at me now, and her eyes were kind even though her words were a blade. "I'm so sorry. I can see you're a good person, and I am so sorry to do this to you on this day. But you need to know before you walk through that door."

She opened the folder against her chest.

"Andrei is my husband. We married eight years ago. And despite what he may have told you — despite what he may have told himself — we are still legally married. Our divorce was never finalized. He abandoned the process. He abandoned me. But on paper, in the eyes of the law, he is still my husband."

The bouquet felt very heavy in my hands.

"That's not possible," I said. But I was looking at Andrei's face, and his face was telling me it was very possible.

"He told you he left a complicated life behind," Oksana said gently. "He didn't leave it. He just stopped answering it. If you marry him today, it isn't a marriage. It's bigamy. And you would be the one blindsided when it unravels."

She held out the folder. The marriage certificate. The abandoned divorce filing. Dates. Stamps. Proof.

I turned to the man I'd been about to marry. The man whose hand had already let go of mine.

"Is it true?" I asked.

I keep coming back to the small things, because that's where the lie lived.

"I left a complicated life behind." "I don't like to talk about my past." "Blue is my favorite color." I'd taken every one of those as charm, as depth, as a wounded man learning to trust again. I'd been so proud of myself for being patient, for not prying, for "respecting his privacy."

I'd respected my way right up the steps to a marriage that would have been a crime.

And the cruelest part wasn't even Oksana's folder. It was watching his hand let go of mine before she finished her first sentence. Because a man who was innocent, a man caught in a genuine misunderstanding, holds on tighter. Andrei let go. His body knew the truth before his mouth could shape another lie.

And what Andrei said on those courthouse steps — and what I did with my little bouquet and my blue dress and the wreckage of the happiest day of my life — is the part I'm still learning how to live with.
